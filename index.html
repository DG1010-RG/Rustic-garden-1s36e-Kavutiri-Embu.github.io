▸ Kenya National Context

Kenya's safe annual recharge (sustainable yield) is estimated at ~193 × 10⁶ m³/yr, roughly 8–9% of total precipitation. FAO 2022 data record ~0.53 km³/yr in total national groundwater withdrawal — a figure that highlights the scale of extraction relative to what the land can safely replenish.

Under the feet of Kwa Nyuki residents there is a vast amount of water stored in the soil and rocks. Scientists estimate that about 1.5 billion cubic metres of water has been removed from beneath Nairobi since the 1950s — like hundreds of thousands of large swimming pools of water.

Think of the ground as a giant sponge or underground lake. Every year, rain helps refill that sponge a little. Nairobi receives roughly 1,000 mm of rainfall per year, but only about 5% of that soaks down to reach the underground water. That means roughly 50 litres per square metre of ground each year — approximately 50 one-litre bottles of water per square metre.

Meanwhile, thousands of wells pump water out of the ground every day. Over 7,000 wells supply groundwater across the city, contributing roughly one-fifth of Nairobi's water supply. Because pumping greatly exceeds recharge, the water level underground has been falling — measured at about half a metre per year in some locations.

It is like drinking from a large water jug faster than it can be refilled. If this continues, the jug — our underground water — gets emptier and emptier.

▸ What Can Help

Careful extraction limits, tree planting to increase infiltration, and building small water-retention features in the landscape can slow — and eventually reverse — the decline. Every extra litre that soaks into the ground instead of running off adds to the community's long-term water security.

Document 03 — Satellite Analysis · Masii, Machakos

Where Is
The Water?

A field investigation using Sentinel-2 Normalized Difference Moisture Index (NDMI) satellite imagery, conducted by Douglas Gitari. The satellite is a witness that cannot lie — it only sees water where water truly exists.

🛰
The Topographic Sink — 86 m East of the Homestead

While the rest of the farm dries within 3–6 days of rainfall, one zone held detectable moisture for 8–9 days, with traces lingering until January 17th. This anomalous persistence is not random — it is structurally controlled. Water flows laterally beneath the surface from higher elevations and pools in this topographic low. A subsurface hardpan or clay lens acts as a natural liner, refusing to let water sink beyond root depth. In Machakos, evaporation should clear surface water in under a day — yet here, one day stretched to nine. That is a 20-fold blessing by hydrological standards.

▸ Hydrological Interpretation

Persistent dark-toned areas indicate moisture retention controlled by local soil and topographic conditions. If the same locations remain dark across multiple satellite passes, moisture is structurally controlled, not rainfall-driven. Likely causes: subsurface flow paths, soil texture contrasts, and shallow groundwater influence.

Document 03 — Agronomic Vision

The Farm
Systems Plan

Armed with satellite intelligence, the 86-metre eastern zone becomes the beating heart of a regenerative farm system — from aquaculture pond to medicinal herb production and legacy mango orchard.

700K
Litres / Year — Harvestable Rainfall

With a one-hectare catchment area and Machakos's typical rainfall, this land is capable of harvesting approximately 700,000 litres of water annually. By deepening the depression at the 86 m mark, water moves from the surface — where evaporation steals it — into a deep, protected volume.

A professional pond liner or heavily compacted clay core ensures that Tilapia or Catfish swim safely through the scorching heat of February and August. The soil already resists drainage — engineering merely amplifies what nature placed there.
